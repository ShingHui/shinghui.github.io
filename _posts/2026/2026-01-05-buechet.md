<!-- ===================== INSPIRATION ===================== -->
<h1 class="section-title">Inspiration</h1>
<p class="section-lead">Where creativity meets perception</p>

<div class="inspiration-grid">

  <div class="inspiration-body">
    <p>The project draws inspiration from "Tainan 400, Here We Meet", a celebration of Tainan's four centuries of cultural and historical fusion.</p>
    <p>I was inspired by how the Beuchet Chair Illusion challenges perception — just as Tainan's identity intertwines past and present, illusion and reality.</p>
    <p>Since the project required an interactive and visually striking design that could leave a strong impression on viewers, our instructor encouraged us to explore perceptual experiences.</p>
    <p>After studying various interactive artworks, I decided to reinterpret the concept of illusion through the traditional "Chair 400."</p>
  </div>

  <div class="inspiration-quote">
    <p>"The design incorporates symbolic elements from <em>Bischofia javanica</em> and <em>Punica granatum</em> — two species deeply rooted in Tainan's 400-year heritage — combined with local wood inlay and carving craftsmanship to express the city's enduring cultural essence."</p>
  </div>

  <div class="inspiration-image">
    <img src="/assets/img/portfolio/tainan-400.jpg" alt="chair 400">
  </div>

<!-- ===================== RESEARCH ===================== -->
<h1 class="section-title">Research Foundation</h1>
<p class="section-lead">To better understand the mechanism behind optical illusions, my research focused on three key references:</p>

<div class="research-grid">

  <div class="research-card">
    <i class="fas fa-eye"></i>
    <h3>"Your Amazing Brain" Exhibition (2022)</h3>
    <p>An interactive neuroscience exhibition developed by the Welcome Centre for Integrative Neuroimaging at the University of Oxford and Banbury Museum & Gallery. It explored perceptual and optical illusions to demonstrate how the brain interprets scale, distance, and visual cues.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="research-card">
    <i class="fas fa-book-open"></i>
    <h3>Jean Beuchet (1963)</h3>
    <p>The original study that introduced the Beuchet Chair illusion, analyzing how perceived distance affects apparent size. This research established the foundational principle behind the illusion used in this project.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="research-card">
    <i class="fas fa-pen"></i>
    <h3>Wiseman, R. (2016)</h3>
    <p>A contemporary reinterpretation of the Beuchet Chair illusion that refined viewing conditions and structural composition. This article inspired me to explore how the same perceptual principle could be adapted into a cultural context.</p>
  </div>

</div>

<div class="challenge-block" style="margin-bottom:30px;">
  <h4><i class="fas fa-exclamation-triangle"></i> The Challenge</h4>
  <p>
    Initially, the lack of clear dimensional data and viewing angles made it difficult to determine where to begin. Online sources provided limited technical information, leaving much of the process reliant on <strong>trial and error</strong>.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="breakthrough-block">
  <h4><i class="fas fa-cogs"></i> Technical Breakthroughs</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>
      <strong>Finding the Ratio:</strong> Through analyzing video footage, I discovered that a <strong>1:3 scale</strong> successfully produced the intended illusion. (Future exploration: 1:2 scale or alternative angles).
    </li>
    <li>
      <strong>Structural Simplification:</strong> Refining the structure from <strong>four legs to three</strong> simplified the overall system, eliminating the need for complex chassis calculations.
    </li>
  </ul>
</div>

<div class="takeaway-block">
  <h4><i class="fas fa-lightbulb"></i> Key Takeaway</h4>
  <p>
    This project highlighted the importance of <strong>adaptability and systematic experimentation</strong> when translating perceptual theory into physical form.
  </p>
